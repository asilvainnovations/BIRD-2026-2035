Chapter 8
Policy Recommendations and the Way Forward
From Analysis to Action: High-Leverage Interventions for Policymakers, Planners, and Investors

The Bangsamoro Investment Roadmap Development (BIRD) 2026–2035, as articulated across the preceding seven chapters, represents a comprehensive, ecosystem-level strategy for transforming BARMM from a post-conflict recovery region into a premier hub for resilient, ethical, and sustainable growth. The Integrated Ecosystem Development Strategy (IEDS) posits that economic transformation requires the synchronized evolution of the Bangsamoro Economic and Investment Ecosystem (BEIE) clusters: Foundations, Transformers, Enablers, Connectors, and Financiers. The Balanced Scorecard translates this strategy into 27 measurable objectives across four perspectives. The Implementation Roadmap sequences ₱120–160 billion in investments across three phases. The MEL framework ensures adaptive management and accountability.

Yet strategy, however elegant, remains inert without execution. This final chapter bridges the gap between analysis and action by presenting high-leverage policy recommendations categorized by stakeholder group. These recommendations are not aspirational wish lists; they are derived directly from the SWOT diagnostic (Chapter 3), the systems archetype analysis (Chapter 3), the strategic options evaluation (Chapter 4), the KPI benchmarking (Chapter 5), the BSC causal linkages (Chapter 6), and the phased implementation roadmap (Chapter 7). Each recommendation specifies the actor, action, rationale, and expected impact—ensuring clarity of responsibility and measurability of outcomes.

The chapter concludes with a reflection on the window of opportunity before the Bangsamoro people and a call to collective action that honors both the analytical rigor of this roadmap and the moral imperative of inclusive development.

8.1 For Policymakers: Bangsamoro Parliament, Chief Minister, and Executive Leadership

The legislative and executive leadership of BARMM holds the unique authority to enact the enabling laws, allocate the public resources, and establish the institutional architecture that no other stakeholder can provide. Their decisions in the first 24 months of this roadmap will determine whether the IEDS achieves lift-off or stalls on the runway. The following four recommendations address the highest-leverage legislative and policy actions required.

Recommendation 8.1: Fast-Track the Bangsamoro Forestry Code and Operationalize Green Economy Mechanisms

Actor: Bangsamoro Parliament (primary); Chief Minister's Office (supporting); MENRE (implementing)

Action: Enact the Bangsamoro Forestry Code by Q1 2027 and immediately operationalize the Draft MENRE-MILG Joint Memorandum Circular (JMC) No. 2026-01.

Rationale: The Forestry Code is the critical legislative key to monetizing BARMM's environmental assets. Without it, the Payment for Ecosystem Services (PES), carbon credit programs, and eco-tourism user fees envisioned in the IEDS have no legal foundation. Early implementation positions BARMM to capture the "first-mover" advantage in the regional carbon market before competitors (e.g., Indonesia, Malaysia) mature their frameworks (Silva, 2026). The systems analysis in Chapter 3 identified the "Tragedy of the Commons" as a dominant archetype in BARMM's natural resource management; the Forestry Code is the structural intervention that transforms open-access resources into governed assets.

Expected Impact: Generation of ₱200–500 million annual revenue for LGUs and communities from avoided deforestation and ecosystem services by 2035. This directly supports BSC Financial objective F4 (green economy revenue) and Stakeholder objective S4 (poverty reduction).

BSC Link: LP5 → P6 → F4 → S4

Recommendation 8.2: Institutionalize the Bangsamoro Investment Command Center (BICC)

Actor: Chief Minister's Office; MTIT-BBOI (host ministry)

Action: Establish the BICC as a permanent unit within MTIT-BBOI through executive order or legislative act, empowered with cross-agency coordination authority and a digital dashboard for real-time tracking of investment projects. Allocate ₱30 million for Year 1 setup (office, digital dashboard, MOA drafting).

Rationale: Fragmentation of programs across ministries remains the single greatest systemic bottleneck to implementation. The BICC directly addresses the "Tragedy of the Commons" archetype by ensuring that infrastructure projects (e.g., roads) align with production zones (e.g., agriculture) and market access points (e.g., ports), thereby synchronizing the BEIE clusters. Without centralized coordination, individual ministries will continue optimizing their own performance at the expense of ecosystem coherence (Chapter 3, Systems Mapping).

Expected Impact: Reduction in inter-agency project delays by 40%; improvement in inter-agency coordination score from 5.5/10 to 7.5/10 by 2028. Directly supports BSC Internal Process objective P5 (BMOA synchronization).

BSC Link: LP3 → P5 → P4 → F2

Recommendation 8.3: Accelerate the Bangsamoro Halal Industry Development Plan (BHIDP)

Actor: MTIT-BHB (primary); Parliament (budgetary support); BSP (Islamic finance alignment)

Action: Mandate the full operationalization of the Bangsamoro Halal Board (BHB) with OIC/SMIIC-aligned certification standards. Allocate ₱85 million for QA laboratory establishment in Cotabato and Marawi, halal training academy development, and international MRA negotiation.

Rationale: Halal certification is the gateway to the $2.3 trillion global halal market. Reducing certification processing time from the current 45–60 days to the international benchmark of 21 days is critical for MSME competitiveness (MTIT-BARMM, 2025). The SWOT analysis (Chapter 3) identified halal certification as the highest-readiness strength (RI = 4.2), making it the optimal "quick win" for building investor confidence and export revenue. The BSC causal chain (Chapter 6) maps halal expertise (L1) through certification processes (P3) to MSME empowerment (S3) and export revenue (F3).

Expected Impact: 2,000 halal-certified MSMEs by 2030; 5,000+ by 2035. Export value from <₱10B to ₱40B+. Directly supports BSC objectives L1, P3, S3, and F3.

BSC Link: LP1 → L1 → P3 → S3 → F3

Recommendation 8.4: Enact the Digital Governance and Connectivity Act

Actor: Bangsamoro Parliament (primary); MICT (technical support)

Action: Legislate data privacy standards and mandate the creation of the Bangsamoro Digital Transformation Authority (BDTA) to oversee the BEGMP implementation. Allocate ₱150 million for Phase 1 digital infrastructure rollout across all seven provinces.

Rationale: Digital infrastructure is a binding constraint for archipelagic provinces like Tawi-Tawi and Basilan. The provincial diagnostics (Chapter 2) reveal that broadband connectivity below 30% severely limits the ability of remote provinces to participate in digital supply chains, access financial services, and deliver e-government services. The 1-day digital business registration system (BSC P1) is the front-door through which investors enter BARMM; without digital backbone, it cannot function.

Expected Impact: Business registration time reduced from multiple days to 1 day (digital) by Q4 2026. Broadband connectivity from <30% to 60% by 2030. Directly supports BSC objectives L4, P1, P2, and S1.

BSC Link: LP3 → L4 → P1 → S1 → F1

8.2 For Development and Investment Planners: BMOAs, Provincial LGUs, and Agencies

Planners at the ministry, provincial, and municipal levels are the operational engine of the IEDS. Their shift from sectoral silos to ecosystem-based planning determines whether the BEIE synchronization envisioned in Chapter 4 becomes operational reality or remains conceptual aspiration. The following three recommendations target the planning practices, provincial interventions, and MSME support systems required.

Recommendation 8.5: Prioritize "Infrastructure-First" Sequencing in Capital Budgeting

Actor: BPDA (primary); MPW, MENRE, MAFAR (implementing); Provincial LGUs (local alignment)

Action: Align the Multi-Year Investment Plan (MYIP) and Annual Investment Program (AIP) with the BEIE framework by prioritizing "Enablers" that unlock "Foundations" and "Transformers." Specifically: prioritize farm-to-market roads, cold chain facilities, and renewable energy mini-grids over standalone administrative buildings. Apply the Coherent Sequencing Logic as a mandatory criterion in project evaluation.

Rationale: Data indicates that 20–40% of agricultural output is lost due to post-harvest inefficiencies (MFBM, 2025). Investing in logistics infrastructure reduces these losses, directly increasing farmer and fisherfolk income without necessarily increasing production volume. The systems analysis (Chapter 3) identified the "Limits to Growth" archetype where inadequate infrastructure creates a binding constraint (B1 loop) that caps economic output regardless of production investment. Breaking this ceiling requires infrastructure-first sequencing.

Expected Impact: Post-harvest losses reduced from 35% to 15% by 2030; infrastructure budget execution rate from <80% to >90% by 2035. Directly supports BSC objectives P4 and F2.

BSC Link: LP2 → P4 → F2

Recommendation 8.6: Implement Province-Specific Value Chain Interventions

Actor: Provincial LGUs (primary); BBOI-MTIT (facilitation); MAFAR, SPDA (technical support)

Action: Tailor interventions to each province's comparative advantage, with mandatory PAP alignment to provincial BEIE profiles:

Maguindanao del Norte: Operationalize the Bangsamoro Halal Park in Matanog. Develop MSME linkages to create a clustered manufacturing hub for halal food and cosmetics. Budget: ₱1,500M.

Maguindanao del Sur: Implement climate-smart agriculture with irrigation modernization and drought-resistant varieties. Prioritize post-harvest facilities for rice and corn. Budget: ₱300M.

Basilan: Leverage the ASG-free "Peace Dividend" by fast-tracking the Rubber Industry Development Program. Modernize tapping techniques and establish TSR processing facilities. Budget: ₱180M.

Tawi-Tawi: Address the digital divide by expanding broadband connectivity for the seaweed industry. Invest in marine protected areas to sustain "Seaweed Capital" status. Budget: ₱200M.

Lanao del Sur: Expand the agro-industrial corridor initiated by DoleFil. Support MSME transition from raw material suppliers to certified halal processors. Budget: ₱250M.

Sulu: Develop cultural heritage tourism infrastructure linking historical sites with halal-friendly hospitality. Budget: ₱100M.

Special Geographic Area: Coordinate with parent provinces for integrated service delivery and investment facilitation. Budget: ₱50M.

Rationale: The provincial diagnostics (Chapter 2) revealed a 3.9 percentage point inter-provincial growth disparity, with Lanao del Sur and Maguindanao del Norte attracting disproportionate investment while Tawi-Tawi and Sulu lag behind. The BSC Stakeholder objective S7 (provincial equity) mandates affirmative intervention to prevent the "Success to the Successful" archetype from widening this gap. Province-specific interventions ensure that the IEDS reaches all Bangsamoro citizens, not just those in the most developed areas.

Expected Impact: Inter-provincial growth disparity reduced from 3.9 pp to <2.5 pp by 2030 and <1.5 pp by 2035. Directly supports BSC objective S7.

BSC Link: LP2 → S7 → S4 → F2

Recommendation 8.7: Strengthen MSME Digitalization and Resilience

Actor: MTIT-ETD (primary); MICT (digital support); CARD Islamic Bank (financial inclusion); TESDA (skills)

Action: Fully implement the Bangsamoro MSME Development Plan (BMSMED) 2026–2032 by establishing Negosyo Centers in all seven provincial capitals, integrating MSMEs into digital supply chains, and deploying digital Islamic microfinance pilots in three provinces.

Rationale: Digitalization allows MSMEs to access wider markets and financial services. The BMSMED emphasizes that digital transformation is a major pivot for revitalizing the sector, particularly for women and youth entrepreneurs (MTIT-ETD, 2025). With approximately 78% of BARMM's workforce in the informal sector, MSME formalization and digitalization are essential for poverty reduction (BSC S4) and employment creation (BSC S6). The "Inclusive Development" causal pathway (Chapter 6) links functional literacy (L5) and TESDA alignment (L7) through MSME empowerment to poverty reduction.

Expected Impact: 5,000+ formalized MSMEs by 2030; 20,000+ quality jobs created (BOI-registered firms). Financial inclusion rate from ~38% to 55% by 2030. Directly supports BSC objectives S3, S4, S5, S6, and L7.

BSC Link: LP1 + LP4 → L5 + L7 → S3 + S5 + S6 → S4 → F2

8.3 For Investors: Domestic, Foreign, and Development Partners

Investors—whether domestic conglomerates, foreign multinationals, or development finance institutions—are the capital catalysts that transform the IEDS from a government plan into a market reality. BARMM offers distinct competitive advantages that are not fully priced into current investment flows: authentic cultural credibility in the $2.3 trillion halal market, abundant raw materials (rubber, coconut, seaweed, fisheries), and a young, growing population. The following four recommendations identify priority investment corridors where these advantages translate into risk-adjusted returns.

Recommendation 8.8: Invest in the Halal Economy and Agro-Processing

Target Investors: Domestic food processors, UAE/GCC halal conglomerates, ASEAN agribusiness, private equity (food & beverage)

Action: Establish processing facilities for high-value halal products (carrageenan, virgin coconut oil, rubber latex concentrate, halal-certified seafood) rather than raw commodity trading. Utilize the Bangsamoro Halal Park (Matanog) and Polloc Freeport Economic Zone as ready-made infrastructure platforms.

Rationale: Global halal demand is growing at 6–8% annually, and BARMM offers authentic cultural credibility combined with abundant raw materials. The 7-criteria scoring matrix (Chapter 4) ranked Halal Economy Leadership as the highest-scoring strategic option due to its alignment with BEIE clusters, multiplier effects, and moral governance resonance. The BSC "Halal Economy Value Chain" causal pathway (Chapter 6) maps this investment through certification (P3) and MSME empowerment (S3) to export revenue (F3) and GRDP growth (F2).

Expected Return: 15–25% IRR on halal processing facilities; access to OIC/SMIIC-compliant markets via BHB certification. Market size: $2.3 trillion global halal economy.

BSC Link: LP1 → P3 → S3 → F3 → F2

Recommendation 8.9: Capitalize on Renewable Energy and Green Infrastructure

Target Investors: IPP developers, green infrastructure funds, climate finance institutions (GCF, ADB), Islamic green sukuk investors

Action: Explore Public-Private Partnerships (PPPs) for grid interconnection (ZBIP completion), solar farms for agro-industrial zones, biomass plants (coconut husk, rice husk), and mini-hydro projects in Lanao del Sur.

Rationale: BARMM aims for 85% renewable energy mix by 2035. The energy deficit—25% of areas unserved—represents both a binding constraint and an investment opportunity. The ZBIP alone (₱6.67B) addresses the critical path for Basilan electrification. The BSEMP identifies significant untapped potential for solar (5.5 kWh/m²/day insolation) and biomass (300,000+ tons/year agricultural residue). Climate finance through green sukuk offers Shariah-compliant capital structures that align with BARMM's ethical investment mandate.

Expected Return: 12–18% IRR on solar/biomass IPPs; carbon credit revenue (₱100–500M annually by 2035) provides supplementary income stream.

BSC Link: LP2 → P4 → F2; LP5 → P6 → F4

Recommendation 8.10: Develop Sustainable Tourism and Cultural Heritage Assets

Target Investors: Hospitality groups, eco-tourism developers, cultural heritage investors, ASEAN tourism funds

Action: Develop "Halal-friendly" tourism infrastructure—including hotels, resorts, and transport services catering to Muslim travelers—at priority sites: Tawi-Tawi marine sanctuaries, Sulu historical sites, Lanao del Sur eco-tourism corridor, and Lake Lanao cultural circuit.

Rationale: The Bangsamoro Tourism Development Plan (BTDP) 2024–2033 identifies eco-tourism and cultural heritage as key growth drivers. The BSC targets 1 million annual tourist arrivals by 2030 and 3 million by 2035. Halal tourism is the fastest-growing segment globally (8.3% CAGR), and BARMM's Islamic cultural heritage provides authentic differentiation from secular competitors. The Lake Lanao eco-tourism corridor alone could generate ₱500M+ annual revenue while preserving the watershed that sustains 500,000 people.

Expected Return: 18–28% IRR on premium halal-friendly resorts; government incentives through CREATE MORE Act and BARMM investment code.

BSC Link: LP5 → P6 → F4 → F2

Recommendation 8.11: Expand Islamic Finance and Fintech Services

Target Investors: Islamic banks, fintech platforms, takaful providers, microfinance institutions, venture capital (fintech)

Action: Expand branch networks and digital wallets to underserved municipalities. Launch takaful (Islamic insurance) products for agriculture and climate risk, murabahah financing for MSMEs, and green sukuk for renewable energy projects.

Rationale: Formal financial service penetration in BARMM is among the lowest nationally. The 7-criteria matrix (Chapter 4) ranked Islamic Finance Ecosystem Development as a top-tier strategic option due to its alignment with moral governance, social inclusion, and financial deepening objectives. The BSC "Islamic Finance Ecosystem" causal pathway (Chapter 6) links Islamic finance professionals (L2) through Shariah-compliant products (S5) to capital channeling (F5) and new investment generation (F1). The Al-Amanah Islamic Investment Bank of the Philippines, with 81 branches nationwide, provides an existing platform for BARMM-focused expansion.

Expected Return: 10–16% IRR on Islamic microfinance portfolios; green sukuk at competitive rates (6–8%). First-mover advantage in the underserved Bangsamoro market.

BSC Link: LP4 → L2 → S5 → F5 → F1

8.4 Conclusion and the Way Forward

The Bangsamoro Investment Roadmap Development 2026–2035 is not merely a planning document. It is a theory of change grounded in systems thinking, calibrated against global benchmarks, and operationalized through a measurement architecture that links every training program, every infrastructure project, and every halal certificate to a coherent causal chain culminating in the vision of a ₱550 billion economy with poverty below 20%.

The eight chapters of this publication have traced a logical progression: from context (Chapter 2) to diagnosis (Chapter 3) to strategy (Chapter 4) to metrics (Chapter 5) to scorecard (Chapter 6) to implementation (Chapter 7) to action (Chapter 8). Each chapter builds upon the previous, creating an integrated whole that is greater than the sum of its analytical parts.

The Decisive Decade

The 10-year horizon is not arbitrary. It matches the remaining term of the Bangsamoro Organic Law's transition period, aligns with the Philippine Development Plan 2023–2028 and its successor, and corresponds to the SMIIC Strategic Plan 2021–2030 implementation phase. Three electoral cycles, two Philippine administrations, and one generation of Bangsamoro youth will pass during this decade. The choices made in these first years—whether to enact the Forestry Code, whether to fund the ZBIP, whether to operationalize the BHB—will determine whether BARMM realizes its extraordinary potential or remains trapped in the systems patterns that have constrained its development for decades.

The Moral Imperative

Beyond the analytics, beyond the metrics, beyond the scorecards, lies a moral imperative. The Bangsamoro people have endured decades of conflict, displacement, and underdevelopment. The peace agreement that created BARMM was not merely a political settlement; it was a covenant that promised a better future. This roadmap honors that covenant by providing the technical architecture for inclusive, sustainable, and ethical growth. Moral Governance is not a slogan; it is the operating system that ensures every peso of investment serves community welfare, every infrastructure project protects environmental assets, and every economic gain is shared across all seven provinces—not just the most fortunate.

The Window of Opportunity

The convergence of factors creating this opportunity is historically unique: the political will of the Bangsamoro government, the policy space of autonomous governance, the maturing of global halal markets, the availability of climate finance, the advancing digital transformation, and the demonstrated commitment of development partners. This window will not remain open indefinitely. Regional competitors are advancing their own halal strategies. Climate finance allocations are time-bound. The demographic dividend of BARMM's young population will not last forever.

"By enacting enabling laws, synchronizing infrastructure with production, and attracting strategic investments, the Bangsamoro can transition from a post-conflict recovery region to a premier hub for resilient, ethical, and sustainable growth. The window of opportunity is open; decisive action is now required."

A Shared Responsibility

The realization of BIRD 2026–2035 is not the responsibility of any single actor. It requires the Parliament to enact enabling laws. The Chief Minister to champion the IEDS across government. Ministries to synchronize their programs through the BICC. Provincial LGUs to deliver local interventions with community ownership. Investors to channel capital into productive enterprise. Development partners to provide technical assistance and co-financing. Civil society to hold all actors accountable. And the Bangsamoro people—farmers, fisherfolk, entrepreneurs, students, artisans—to seize the opportunities this roadmap creates.

The Final Word

This publication provides the roadmap. The data, the analysis, the frameworks, the metrics, and the recommendations are all here. What cannot be captured in these pages—what no scorecard can measure and no causal diagram can represent—is the resolve of a people who have endured and are now ready to build. That resolve is the ultimate leverage point. All the systems thinking, all the strategic planning, and all the investment roadmapping are merely tools in service of a singular purpose: the emergence of a Bangsamoro that is prosperous, equitable, and at peace with itself and its neighbors.

The Emerging Bangsamoro is not a destination.
It is a journey that begins now.
